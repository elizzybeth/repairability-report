## What the recent videos have in common

The best recent iFixit teardown videos open with a sharp thesis, not a generic "today we are opening a gadget" setup. The title usually does part of the work already: it frames a tension, a question, or a verdict. The first paragraph then immediately tells the viewer why the device matters and what repairability angle makes it worth caring about.

The voice is conversational, a little mischievous, and always grounded in evidence. The scripts use short declarative sentences, occasional jokes, and strong contrast words: good news, bad news, but, however, and on the other hand. They do not linger on setup.

## Structural pattern

1. Hook in one or two sentences.
2. Explain why the device is interesting to repair people.
3. Establish the repair question that the teardown will answer.
4. Move quickly into the opening disassembly step.
5. Use the teardown itself as the body of the story.
6. Reserve the broadest argument for the end, once the device evidence is on screen.

The teardown portion is usually paced as a chain of small discoveries. Each step answers a practical question:
- How hard is it to open?
- What comes out first?
- What is modular?
- What is soldered or glued?
- What seems intentionally repair-friendly?
- What looks good until you try to service it?

## Tone and language

The tone is light, but not sloppy. The strongest scripts sound like a knowledgeable repairer talking to other repairers.

Common traits:
- A clear point of view from sentence one.
- Plain language for non-specialists, with technical detail when it matters.
- Humor that lands because it is attached to a real repair observation.
- No corporate neutrality when the evidence is bad.
- Praise that is specific, not generic.

## How the teardown is narrated

Recent videos often describe the device in the present tense as parts come off:
- what is accessible now,
- what is hidden under the previous layer,
- what the next failure point is likely to be,
- what a real repair would require in practice.

That keeps the script useful for b-roll. The viewer can follow the physical teardown without the narration drifting away into policy talk.

## How scores are handled

Scores are usually not introduced as abstract numbers. They are earned by what the teardown reveals. The score appears after the audience has seen enough evidence to understand it.

When iFixit compares a score to a manufacturer claim, the script works best when it:
- names the claim plainly,
- shows the independent evidence,
- explains the missing repair friction,
- and then states the iFixit score as the answer to the teardown question.

## Best fit for the ASUS video

For the ASUS video, the script should:
- open with the 10/10 claim and the question "Is it?"
- explain iFixit's category-based scoring before the teardown starts
- make the laptop-vs-tablet choice part of the story, not an aside
- stay with the disassembly for most of the runtime
- use the intro and conclusion for the broader EU-scoring critique
- keep the systemic argument tied to what the viewer just saw in the device

The ASUS piece should feel like a standard iFixit teardown first, and a policy critique second. That keeps it in family with the recent videos while still giving the report context room to breathe.

## ASUS-specific angle

This one should lean harder on the practical repair path than on abstract scoring theory.

The key beat is: ASUS has some genuinely good design choices, but the repair story collapses when you look at the whole path from diagnosis to parts to reassembly. That means the script should keep returning to concrete questions:
- Can an owner find the manual?
- Can they identify the right part?
- Can they buy the part?
- Can they diagnose the fault without a working machine?
- Does the score still look good once those answers are no?

The strongest version of the video will treat the score as the result of that path, not a detached verdict.
