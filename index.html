Investigative report

The EU is raising the bar on repair. Our data shows where it still falls short.

Nearly a year after the EU's smartphone repairability labels became law, iFixit reviewed public product records, retailer listings, store shelves, and one high-scoring ASUS device. Our analysis found missing repair labels, empty parts-price links, Temu links in official records, and a 10/10 claim that deserves public proof.

Core case

Europe is ahead. That is exactly why these gaps matter.

Repair policy is moving all over the world. The US has real state-level Right to Repair wins, and advocates are pushing repair access, parts availability, and repairability information across Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Brazil, India, Taiwan, Europe, and beyond. Europe is ahead because it is the only place where repairability labels, spare-parts access, and repair-information rules are all far enough along to stress-test in public. So we went looking at the places where those rules meet real life: public score records, European Product Registry for Energy Labelling (EPREL) product sheets, retailer listings, store shelves, ASUS support pages, and a professional repairer's attempt to get the information he is supposed to have.

Repairability scoring is one of the reforms we are most excited about because it turns repair into a point-of-sale comparison. A score gives consumers a way to compare products, gives journalists a concrete claim to test, and gives regulators a public promise to enforce. The score only works if the evidence behind it is visible enough to check.

France moved first. Its repairability index took effect on January 1, 2021 for five categories of electronic devices sold in France, after a 2019 law required clear repairability information at the point of sale. Manufacturers calculate the score, sellers display it, and the 0-to-10 grade is built from criteria such as documentation, disassembly, spare-parts availability, and spare-parts prices.

The EU followed with smartphone and tablet repairability classes on the energy label, in force from June 20, 2025. Those labels run from A to E and are based on factors including disassembly depth, fasteners, tools, spare-parts availability, software updates, and repair information.

This report stress-tests implementation across several repair reforms. Some findings are about France's national score. Others are about EU energy labels, ecodesign rules, EPREL records, retailer display, and repair-information access. The pattern is the same: a repair rule is only useful if people can see it, verify it, and use it. Too often, market surveillance is not catching inflated scores or bad information, the link goes nowhere useful, the evidence is hard to check, or the label never reaches the shopper.

Perfect scores need proof

ASUS's dedicated French repairability site publishes a 10/10 scorecard for the ROG Flow Z13 GZ302EA. But our teardown found soldered components, parts-access friction, and documentation gaps that make a perfect score hard to square without audit-ready evidence.

Broken links

EPREL records point consumers to empty fields, "see manual" notes, generic support pages, AliExpress, and Temu for legally relevant repair information.

Missing at retail

We found major online and physical retailers failing to display required smartphone energy and repairability labels.

Taken together, these findings argue for improving the repair-label system, not retreating from it. Score claims need evidence, official records need working links, and retailers need to show repair information where people actually shop. Market regulators should treat EPREL records and retailer disclosures as live compliance targets, not static paperwork. They should check whether the public data matches the product on sale, whether the links resolve to useful repair information, and whether the label is visible at the point of purchase. If the system is going to earn consumer trust, it needs routine enforcement, visible consequences for bad records, and faster correction when manufacturers or retailers get the details wrong.

Perfect score?

ASUS gave the Flow Z13 a 10/10. We put it to the test.

ASUS says the ROG Flow Z13 GZ302EA deserves a 10/10 on the French Repairability Index, and that claim is exactly the kind that begs for a closer look. The scorecard is public, which is useful. The tricky part is that not everything behind a perfect score is equally easy for a consumer, journalist, or independent repair group to verify.

We tried to verify that 10/10 claim from the outside. We could check the public scorecard, the published service manual, the physical design of the device, and the repair/support paths ASUS makes visible online. We could not independently verify every underlying commitment that feeds the French score, such as future parts availability, delivery timelines, or the internal evidence behind some criterion calculations.

The visible evidence gave us reasons to question whether a perfect score was plausible. The device has soldered USB-C ports, soldered RAM and wireless, and a fan trapped under the heatsink. The service manual is public and useful, but it lacks troubleshooting, board diagrams, and a parts list with part numbers. ASUS's official support route also does not clearly show ordinary consumers how to buy parts, and the customer self-repair pathway appears to depend on diagnostic gates, one-component requests, and possible deposits.

So we did the usual iFixit thing: we took the Flow Z13 apart, documented the repair experience, and scored it on our own repairability scale.

Our score was 7.4/10. The Flow Z13 earned most of its points from repairable design choices: a screen-first opening procedure, a screwed-in battery, an accessible SSD hatch, replaceable cameras and modules, and a useful public service manual. It lost points where those strengths did not translate into a complete repair experience: component-level soldering, harder fan access, incomplete documentation, and unclear parts access.

The Flow Z13 is, truth be told, a remarkably repairable device by modern tablet/laptop standards. ASUS made a repairable product, and a perfect repairability score should still be backed by evidence people can check, especially when the score depends partly on manufacturer commitments about future parts availability, delivery timelines, and support pathways.

Two scoring systems can reasonably produce different numbers. A perfect self-declared score still needs to be verifiable, especially when a teardown reveals meaningful repair barriers.

iFixit has been publishing repairability scores since 2011, and our score does a different job from the French Repairability Index score. We consulted on the French system, but the two methods answer slightly different questions. The French score looks at a broader repair ecosystem in France. Our score puts more weight on product design, teardown evidence, and the repair experience available to a self-repairer right now.

Our scoring also uses product-group calibration in a specific way. Instead of simply adding up category weights and letting scores fall wherever they fall, we score a sample of products in a given category, then calibrate the range so the results spread out instead of clustering near the top. That matters here because the Flow Z13 is a real example of why calibrated scoring is useful: it combines genuinely repair-friendly design choices with barriers that keep a perfect score from being self-evident.

The design has real strengths: the screen comes away first, the battery is screwed rather than glued, the SSD is accessible behind a hatch, cameras and several modules are replaceable, and ASUS publishes a useful service manual. But the charging ports are soldered to the motherboard, RAM and wireless are soldered, one fan is trapped under the heatsink, and the official support path does not clearly lead ordinary consumers to buy parts. In other words, the calibration can reward the good design choices without flattening the repair barriers that still matter in practice.

The service manual is public and useful, though it lacks troubleshooting, a parts list with part numbers, and board diagrams. ASUS's official US support path did not expose parts for sale; the separate ASUS parts site appeared legitimate without a clear endorsement from the official support page.

ASUS has a lot of high French repairability scores. Its dedicated French repairability site claims 10/10 scores for the ROG Flow Z13 GZ302EA and the ROG Zephyrus GU605MZ. A broader public dataset contains many 10/10 ASUS entries, concentrated in variants of a small number of model families, so that count does not show a massive pattern across unrelated designs. The bigger issue is that high scores are everywhere: the public French repairability data is heavily clustered near the top.

The support flow matters

A perfect score has to cover more than opening the device. People also need the parts and information required after the product breaks. That is where the Flow Z13 gets more complicated: ASUS has repair documentation and a customer self-repair pathway, but the pathway comes with conditions that ordinary shoppers will not understand from a 10/10 score.

The support language matters because it affects whether repair is practical. If a customer has to pass a diagnostic gate, file separate requests, or put down a deposit tied to a part's market price, that should be visible in the evidence behind a perfect score.

The QR code is the right idea

Putting repair information one scan away is exactly where these rules should go. The Flow Z13's QR code is a genuinely good consumer-facing move: it gives the buyer a visible bridge from the physical product to repair support.

The hard part is making sure that scan takes people to usable repair paths instead of a support maze. In our review, the route from the QR code to actual parts access was still muddy, especially for repair-critical components that a self-repairer would need to identify, price, and buy without becoming an authorized service center.

The Flow Z13 is a useful test case precisely because ASUS has done a lot right. For consumers, a perfect score should start the repairability question, not end it. For journalists and regulators, the practical ask is narrower: perfect self-declared scores should come with criterion-level evidence and a way to check whether parts, documentation, and support commitments are delivered later.

Missing parts prices

Official repair links often do not lead to repair.

A repairability label depends on the underlying repair resources. EPREL records show widespread failure at that layer.

For smartphones and tablets, EU product information sheets are supposed to include web links for repair instructions, spare-part information, and indicative pre-tax spare-part prices for required parts. Those links should take shoppers to a manufacturer, importer, or authorized-representative page where the relevant information is actually available.

In the smartphone and tablet dataset, 39 devices list Temu as the official web link for repair instructions, spare-part information, and pre-tax spare-part pricing. Temu and AliExpress are marketplaces, with weaker accountability, model-specific parts paths, and official repair-channel functions than consumers should get from a regulatory record. Across a broader review of active smartphone records, 5% point to Temu or AliExpress, and 81% of information sheets do not lead to the spare-parts prices required by regulation.

The majority of smartphone records we examined showed no usable parts-pricing path. That matters because shoppers need a clear price link before repair can be a real decision instead of a guess.

These problems affect whether the label produces meaningful price transparency. If a consumer scans a label and finds an empty field, a generic brand homepage, a locked-down support flow, or a Temu or AliExpress link, the regulation has not delivered the information it promised.

The Temu finding is especially damaging because it puts the burden on consumers to navigate marketplace imports, potential duties, and compliance uncertainty, while still appearing in an official repair-information field.

A Temu listing cannot stand in for a repair channel or a parts catalog, and it does not satisfy the consumer-facing purpose of the label.

Spare-parts pricing matters because repair is often a price decision before it is a technical decision. Manufacturers can make repair effectively unavailable by controlling parts access and setting prices so high that replacement feels like the only rational choice. A phone can have a decent repairability label, a published manual, and a replaceable battery, but if the part is impossible to price or costs too much, the practical repair option disappears.

Right to Repair Europe has been especially clear on this point: expensive spare parts push consumers toward replacement, and research cited by the coalition shows many consumers choose replacement when repair costs rise above about 30% of the cost of a new product. Since labor, shipping, diagnostics, and downtime also add cost, the spare part itself has to be affordable enough to leave room for the rest of the repair.

A price link is what turns the label into a real repair choice. Real Right to Repair compliance means parts are available at reasonable prices, with the information needed to use them. If the official record sends shoppers to a blank field, a support queue, or a marketplace listing, it prevents the most basic comparison: can I afford to fix this thing when it breaks?

A score can look good while the links are missing

Below is a screenshot from the EPREL information sheet for an Acer AGM PAD P2W tablet currently on the market. Despite earning a class B repair score, and a perfect spare-parts availability sub-score, the sheet is missing the required links to spare-parts availability, spare-parts prices, and repair instructions.

This is the problem in miniature: the label points to a product sheet, but the product sheet does not reliably point to the repair information and parts prices a shopper needs. A repairability class can look reassuring at the point of sale while the underlying record fails to deliver the evidence that would let someone check the claim.

That distinction matters because the label and the product information sheet do different jobs. The label is the shorthand a shopper sees first, usually in a store or online listing. The product information sheet is supposed to hold the underlying repair details: the links to parts prices, repair instructions, and other supporting information. If the sheet is missing those links, or if the links go to an empty page, a support queue, or some other dead end, the shopper gets the promise of repair information without the information itself. The next failure mode looks more helpful at first glance: the link exists, but it still does not take the shopper to a usable price.

"Contact support" is not price transparency

If the law is supposed to help you compare repair costs before you buy, a generic support-escalation page does not do the job. You need model-specific prices you can actually see, without opening a ticket, proving ownership, or waiting for a support representative to interpret your request.

This is a consumer-protection issue: shoppers need real parts prices before they buy. Spare-parts price transparency is supposed to help consumers understand whether a product will be affordable to keep alive. A support form turns that comparison into a private negotiation. Marketplace links create a different version of the same problem: the path may look public, but it still lacks the accountability consumers should get from an official record.

A brand page can still dump you into a marketplace

Even when the first click looks official, the repair path can end at a marketplace store instead of a manufacturer-backed parts catalog with clear model-specific prices, stable availability, and accountability if the part is wrong or unavailable.

Marketplaces can be useful for all kinds of things, including repair. An official regulatory record should identify a reliable source for the relevant parts and prices instead of sending consumers into a marketplace maze and calling that parts transparency. The fix is straightforward: point the product sheet to the relevant parts and prices.

This is what good looks like

The standard is achievable. Some manufacturers link directly from the information sheet to model-specific parts and prices, which is exactly what a shopper scanning a label should get.

This example matters because it closes the loop opened by the smartphone records: the problem is fixable when the official record points to the page where the required information already lives. That is the baseline consumers need if repairability labels are going to shape real purchase decisions.

The enforcement takeaway is practical: EPREL should not accept any URL-shaped answer as repair transparency. Journalists can test whether links lead to prices, consumers need those prices before purchase, and regulators can validate the fields well enough to catch empty pages, support queues, and marketplace detours.

Missing retail labels

Retailers are not consistently showing mandatory labels.

The label cannot guide purchases if shoppers never see it.

We checked whether smartphone repairability labels were showing up where shoppers actually compare phones: online retailer listings, product pages, retailer filter counts, and store shelves. For mediamarkt.de and saturn.de, two large German consumer-electronics storefronts, we compared the total number of smartphone listings with the number of listings that exposed the required score or label information. On mediamarkt.de, 2,775 of 4,857 smartphone listings lacked a score. On saturn.de, 2,779 of 4,955 did.

We also reviewed MediaMarkt Belgium's online label filters and did an in-store shelf count at a Brussels MediaMarkt. The Belgian online filter implied labels for fewer than half the listed phones, and the Brussels store had 19 of 113 smartphones on display without the required label. The regulation depends on putting the score in front of the shopper, on the product page or on the shelf, where it can actually shape a decision.

Online filters exposed the gap

If a retailer has thousands of phones listed but the energy-label filters only account for part of the catalog, that is a sign shoppers are not consistently seeing the label data they need. Filters do a different job from labels, but they are a useful clue: they show whether the retailer has structured the catalog around the mandatory information.

For a shopper, the practical effect is simple. If many phones are outside the label filters, comparison gets harder, and the repairability class becomes less useful as a shopping tool.

Marketplace listings make display harder to police

On Amazon.de, labels appeared inconsistently. Listings sold by Amazon were more likely to show the label than third-party seller listings, which is exactly the kind of point-of-sale gap regulators need to watch.

That matters because consumers do not experience marketplaces as a set of separate compliance regimes. They search for a phone, see a list of offers, and compare what is shown. If the label appears for one seller but not another, the rule is not reaching the full shopping experience.

The product page is where the label has to show up

A score buried in a database will not help shoppers unless the label appears where the purchase decision is happening, alongside the price, specs, delivery information, and other facts a shopper actually uses.

This is why missing product-page labels are such a direct consumer problem. The repairability class can be technically available in EPREL and still invisible at the moment when it would help someone choose between two phones.

Store shelves matter too

Repairability labels are supposed to work as consumer information instead of paperwork. If the label is missing in the shop window, the shopper cannot use it, even if the same model has a compliant record somewhere online.

Physical retail still matters for phones. People compare devices by picking them up, checking the price card, and asking staff questions. The label needs to be part of that real-world comparison.

Even big-box stores were inconsistent

At a Brussels MediaMarkt, most phones had labels, but not all. That is progress compared with no labels at all, but the rule only works when display is consistent.

The mixed result is useful because it shows both sides of the story. Retailers can display these labels at scale, and many products were labeled. But partial compliance still leaves shoppers comparing labeled and unlabeled products side by side.

Retail display is the part of the system consumers actually touch. These checks give journalists a repeatable method, show consumers why a missing label should be treated as missing information, and give regulators a straightforward audit target: product pages, marketplace offers, filters, and shelves should all surface the mandatory label consistently.

Self-reported scores

Top-heavy public score data makes comparison harder.

Repair scores are supposed to help shoppers tell the difference between products. When public data clusters near the top of the scale, consumers need more context about how those scores were produced and checked.

We looked at two different public windows into the French Repairability Index. The first is data.gouv.fr, France's official open-data portal, where manufacturers and importers can voluntarily publish repairability score data. The second is Boulanger, a major French electronics and appliance retailer, whose product listings show scores consumers may actually encounter while shopping. The comparison puts a voluntary government upload set beside a retailer catalog.

The voluntary data.gouv.fr repairability entries average 9.22 with a 9.3 median, compared with Boulanger-listed products at 7.96 mean and 8.0 median. High scores may be legitimate, but this pattern shows why self-reported public data needs auditing, public evidence, and enough spread to help consumers compare products.

In the voluntary government dataset, 97% of products score 8/10 or higher, and only 8 of 2,173 score below 6.0.

Voluntary reporting and selection bias are likely part of the explanation, and the system still gives companies every reason to claim the highest score they can without aggressive checks on those claims. When the incentives push upward and the guardrails are weak, the result is predictable: public scores bunch at the top.

Some score elements cannot be audited from a teardown alone. For example, a manufacturer's commitment to provide spare parts for a certain duration may require contracts, supply records, or historical fulfillment evidence. That makes market surveillance and consumer redress especially important.

High scores can be real. A public score dataset with little spread still needs stronger context: who reported the score, what evidence supports it, how conflicts are resolved, and what consumers can do if the promised repair access does not appear. Without that context, journalists have to chase the evidence product by product, consumers lose a comparison tool, and regulators inherit a dataset that is hard to trust at a glance.

Repairer access

Repair information is still hard to get, even for professionals.

The EU repair framework also relies on access to practical repair information. Existing access rights still break down in practice.

To test the consumer side of that promise, we reviewed active tumble dryer records in EPREL and opened the model information sheets that are supposed to support the repair label. We checked whether the spare-parts price link took a shopper to visible, relevant price information, or whether it led to something weaker: a login wall, a serial-number gate, a generic support page, an empty field, or a broken path. The goal was simple: if a consumer is comparing dryers before buying one, can they actually see the parts-price information the rules are supposed to surface?

For tumble dryers, 55% of model information sheets did not link to a page where the required spare-parts price information was actually available. Those records show the consumer version of the problem: repair information promised by the system can disappear behind a weak link. Even qualified repairers can hit gates when they ask for the documents and diagnostics needed to complete a repair.

Jacob D'Hollander repairs appliances in Ghent, Belgium. He has the competence, VAT registration, and insurance professional repairers are expected to have. Since last autumn, he has been asking major appliance brands for the service manuals, diagnostics, wiring diagrams, and error codes he needs to do his job.

Some brands delayed. Some refused. One brand reportedly told him that wiring diagrams and diagnostic programs were proprietary, then later said the refusal was a misunderstanding. Other brands gave him access to Tradeplace, a multi-brand appliance service portal, but only for parts ordering. The repair-information tab he had seen in other screenshots was missing from his account. That matters because a repairer can buy the right part and still be blocked without the service manual, diagnostic routine, wiring diagram, or error-code list needed to install it properly.

Right to Repair Europe, iFixit, and allies fought hard for the Right to Repair Directive, Directive (EU) 2024/1799, which becomes applicable from July 31, 2026. It is a real win: it should push manufacturers of covered goods toward repair obligations, reasonable spare-part prices, and fewer hardware or software tricks that block repair. But as Right to Repair Europe explains, coverage is still limited and much depends on product-specific rules, national implementation, and enforcement.

Without indicting a directive that has not yet fully arrived, Jacob's story warns that broader repair rights may underdeliver if existing access rules remain weakly enforced.

Parts-price links still dead-end

Dryers are considered purchases with long ownership costs. If repair costs are supposed to influence a purchase, the model information sheet needs to take shoppers to the relevant prices before they buy.

This screenshot shows the same basic problem we saw in phones and tablets: a required path exists, but it does not land on the required information. For a consumer trying to estimate long-term ownership costs, that is the difference between transparency and a dead end.

A polished support page still needs parts prices

Support pages can be useful for scheduling service, but the legal transparency goal is different: consumers should be able to see spare-parts prices. A page can look helpful and still fail the specific job the regulation needs it to do.

That distinction is easy to miss in a quick compliance check. A brand support hub may have repair language, warranty links, and service options, but unless it exposes the relevant parts prices, it does not give consumers the comparison tool they were promised.

Repair information is still treated as proprietary

Professional repairers cannot diagnose and fix modern appliances with guesswork. When wiring diagrams, error codes, and diagnostic routines are withheld, the legal right exists mostly on paper.

Jacob's case matters because he is a qualified repairer trying to access the technical information needed to serve customers. If those requests can be refused or delayed without consequences, independent repair remains fragile.

Parts access without information access is only half a repair right

Selling parts is good. But if a qualified repairer cannot see the repair-information tools available to others, the repair system is still tilted toward authorized channels.

This is where repair policy becomes very practical. A repairer may be able to buy a part and still lack the diagnostic procedure, wiring diagram, error-code list, or service mode needed to install it confidently. Real access has to include both the parts and the know-how.

Consumer price transparency and professional information access are part of the same repair promise. A consumer needs to know whether a product will be affordable to maintain; a repairer needs the manuals, diagnostics, and parts paths to make that repair real. For regulators, both problems point to the same enforcement question: when the rules say information must be available, who checks that it actually reaches the person who needs it?

What enforcement needs

We're winning. Now let's make repair real.

Ten years ago, repair advocates were still shouting into the void. Now Right to Repair laws and repairability policies are moving around the world, from US state laws to European repair labels and parts-access rules. That is a huge win.

This report should keep that progress in view. Being able to nitpick scorecards, link quality, label placement, and enforcement is a sign that repair has moved from the margins into policy, product pages, store shelves, and public databases. People are demanding the ability to fix their stuff, and lawmakers are listening.

The throughline is simple: every repair policy needs a last-mile check. The score must be backed by evidence, the link must land on useful information, the label must appear at the point of sale, and the repairer must be able to get the documents needed to finish the job.

Policy wins still have to become working repair systems. The same weak points show up again and again: self-declared scores that are hard to audit, public records with bad links, labels missing at the point of sale, parts prices that take too many clicks or never appear, and repair information that qualified professionals still struggle to obtain. Different actors own different pieces of that problem: manufacturers, importers, retailers, database administrators, and market-surveillance authorities.

There is more to come as the Right to Repair Directive becomes applicable from July 31, 2026. We should expect stronger repair obligations for covered products, better access to spare parts at reasonable prices, and more pressure against software or hardware practices that block repair. That is worth celebrating. And it is exactly why the next phase matters: to make the EU repairability approach as effective as it wants to be, perfect scores need evidence, EPREL links need validation, labels need retail enforcement, repairers need fast access, and consumers need redress when promises are not kept.

The stakes are bigger than Europe. Other countries are watching these systems and deciding what to borrow. In the US, lawmakers have considered repair-score ideas in Washington, Maine, New York, and Colorado. Taiwan's Ministry of Environment has piloted a repairability-index approach for phones and laptops after studying France's system. That is exciting, but it also raises the bar for everyone: we cannot copy-paste today's scorecards and expect them to solve repair on their own. Every new system should learn from the last one, tighten the weak spots, and keep ratcheting up toward repair that people can actually use.

For journalists

Interrogate the scores. Ask manufacturers to publish evidence for perfect ratings, test whether official repair links lead anywhere useful, and check whether retailers are showing labels where the law says they must.

For consumers

A high score should still send you to parts, repair instructions, and a visible label where you shop. Perfect claims should come with public proof.

For regulators

The system needs public score evidence, EPREL link validation, retailer checks, consumer redress, direct parts-price links, and enforceable access for repairers.
