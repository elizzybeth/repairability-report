# ASUS ROG Flow Z13 Video Draft

Target tone: light, fun, punchy, but technically grounded.

Target shape: intro and outro carry the policy argument. The teardown itself carries most of the runtime.

Assumed runtime: 8-12 minutes.

## Opening

### 0:00 - Hook

**VO**
ASUS says this ROG Flow Z13 deserves a 10 out of 10 repairability score.

Is it?

That is a pretty big claim for a machine with soldered RAM, soldered ports, and a repair path that looks much better on paper than it does in real life.

**VO**
Today we are tearing down the ASUS ROG Flow Z13 GZ302EA to see what the hardware actually says.

And because the whole point of repair scoring is to help people compare products, we are using iFixit's score here, not the French one. iFixit scores from public, observable evidence and calibrates the rubric to each product category. That matters. A tablet and a laptop do not fail in the same way, so they should not be graded like they do.

**VO**
The French Repair Index, by contrast, depends on manufacturer-submitted information that is not fully public. That makes it impossible for us to audit the score the same way we can audit the hardware.

**VO**
So let's open it up.

### 0:35 - Why this matters

**VO**
This video is about ASUS, but it also points to a bigger problem. If a company can hand itself a perfect score and the evidence behind it is hard to verify, the score stops being a consumer tool and starts becoming a marketing tool.

For the full wider investigation, the report covers three failures: inflated scoring, broken repair-information links, and labels that never show up where people actually buy things.

**VO**
ASUS is the main character here, but it is not the only company in the neighborhood. We found similar problems across other brands and databases too.

## Teardown

### 0:55 - First look

**ON CAMERA**
Here it is. The ASUS ROG Flow Z13. A tablet pretending to be a laptop, or a laptop pretending to be a tablet, depending on which scorecard you want to win.

**VO**
We are going in from the display side first. That already tells you something about the product architecture: the screen is the front door to everything.

**B-ROLL NOTE**
Show the screen separation cleanly. Keep the explanation short and let the opening do the work.

### 1:20 - Screen removal

**VO**
The screen comes away first, and it comes away cleanly. That is a good start.

No heavy glue battle. No heat-soaked drama. No immediate signs that ASUS built this thing to fight you on day one.

**VO**
This is exactly why category matters. A tablet-style device can be more repairable than a clamshell laptop without magically becoming perfect.

**VO**
That is also why we score this as a tablet. On a laptop scorecard, the soldered RAM alone would be a serious problem. On the tablet side, ASUS gets some credit for the architecture it actually built.

### 1:50 - Battery and early access

**VO**
Once the display is off, the battery can be disconnected right away.

It is screwed in, not glued in. Again: good. Practical. A real repair win.

**VO**
But repairability is not a vibes-based score. One good decision does not erase everything else hiding underneath it.

**VO**
The serial number wrinkle starts to matter here too. ASUS says you need a serial number to get easy access to drivers and manuals, and otherwise that path is a bit buried.

### 2:15 - Accessible modules

**VO**
The M.2 drive is accessible without major disassembly.

The rear camera and front-facing camera modules are also right there after the screen comes off.

That is the kind of layout repair people like to see. If a part fails, you want the route to it to be boring.

### 2:45 - What is buried

**VO**
Now the less fun part.

The speakers are buried under the battery, though they are still removable.

The microSD reader is modular, but it sits under several other components.

The I/O board is modular too, but you have to earn it.

**VO**
This is the real ASUS story so far. There is a lot of modularity in the design, but not everything is placed with service in mind.

**VO**
And the repair path is not just about screws. ASUS's Customer Self Repair wording is consumer-unfriendly, and the FAQ that was supposed to explain how to order an SSD replacement is dead.

### 3:25 - Cooling and thermal complexity

**VO**
One of the two fans is trapped under the heatsink, which turns a simple maintenance job into a more annoying one.

And liquid metal on the SoC does not make that any friendlier.

**VO**
You can see the difference between "repairable" and "pleasant to repair" starting to widen here.

### 3:55 - Mainboard

**VO**
Once the case is cleared out, the motherboard comes out fairly cleanly.

The board itself is not the problem. The problem is what ASUS chose to solder to it.

### 4:15 - The deductions

**VO**
The RAM is soldered.

The wireless is soldered.

And all of the ports are soldered.

That is a lot of permanent decisions on a device that wants credit for being repairable.

**VO**
If you were hoping to swap RAM, fix a port, or rescue a board-level fault without replacing the whole board, this is where the machine stops being generous.

**VO**
That is the point where the laptop scorecard would be harshest. On a tablet scorecard, ASUS still does not get all the way to 10 because the parts and support story do not hold up.

### 4:50 - What the manual gets right and wrong

**VO**
ASUS does publish a service manual, and that is good.

But the manual is incomplete in the ways that matter. It does not give you the full troubleshooting help, the parts detail, or the board-level guidance you would want from a genuinely repair-friendly machine.

**VO**
The bigger issue is the repair path. The official support flow does not clearly put ordinary consumers on a clean path to buy parts.

Parts availability is scarce and hard to find across multiple sites, with no clear endorsement from ASUS.

That is especially ugly because some repairs depend on already having a working machine. If RAM or SSD fails, the whole "just use the diagnostic tool" story starts to fall apart.

And for failures like the wireless card, screen, keyboard, or trackpad, the consumer may need external devices just to get through diagnosis and ordering. A repair system should not assume the broken device can already help itself.

**VO**
That is not a minor paperwork problem. If you cannot find the part, or you need the device working to diagnose the problem, the repair may already be over.

### 5:30 - Score callout

**ON CAMERA**
So, tablet or laptop?

For us, this scores as a tablet. That is the more forgiving lens, and even then it is not a 10.

**VO**
The iFixit score comes out to 7.4 out of 10.

That is a solid score for a device with real strengths. It is not a perfect score, and it is not close enough to be ignored.

### 6:10 - Why this is not a 10/10

**VO**
Here is the basic tradeoff.

The device opens well.
The battery is screwed down.
The SSD is accessible.
Some modules are easy to reach.

But the ports are soldered.
The RAM is soldered.
The wireless is soldered.
Some service steps are buried.
And the parts story is weak enough that a lot of the practical repair value leaks away.

**VO**
That is why category-specific scoring matters. The same hardware gets judged by the failures that actually matter for that class of product.

**VO**
ASUS can call it 10/10 in the French system if the paperwork clears.
It still does not become a 10/10 repair experience.

## Broader findings

### 6:55 - Quick montage into the report

**VO**
The ASUS result is the cleanest example, but it is not the only one.

Our broader investigation found three other problems that keep repair scores from functioning properly in the real world.

**VO**
First: manufacturers can post scores that are much higher than independent assessment would support.

Second: official repair databases can point consumers to empty fields, generic support pages, or even marketplaces instead of usable repair information.

Third: even when the label exists, retailers often fail to show it where people are actually deciding what to buy.

**VO**
So the issue is not just whether the score is mathematically neat. The issue is whether the whole system can be trusted end to end.

**VO**
And ASUS is not alone. Samsung-branded and white-label devices show the same temptation to game the system, and brands like Ulefone and ARCHOS show how weak the underlying data can get when the oversight is thin.

## Closing

### 7:40 - What this means

**VO**
If you are in the EU, the obvious ask is enforcement. Put the label on the shelf, put it on the product page, and make the underlying data public enough that independent researchers can check it.

If the score claims perfection, the public should be able to see why.

**VO**
If you are outside the EU, this is the cautionary tale. Repairability scoring is spreading. Other governments are considering it. International standards work is moving too.

Do not copy a system that cannot be audited, that overweights the wrong thing, or that lets the label outrun the evidence.

**VO**
What should we learn from Europe?

Public criteria.
Category-calibrated scoring.
Transparent inputs.
And labels that actually show up where people buy things.

**VO**
If the rules can be gamed, the result is not a repairability label. It is just a nicer-looking sticker.

**ON CAMERA**
ASUS said 10 out of 10.

We got 7.4.

And the difference matters.
