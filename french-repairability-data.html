Self-reported scores

Top-heavy public score data makes comparison harder.

We looked at two different public windows into the French Repairability Index. The first is data.gouv.fr, France's official open-data portal, where manufacturers and importers can voluntarily publish repairability score data. The second is Boulanger, a major French electronics and appliance retailer, whose product listings show scores consumers may actually encounter while shopping.

The comparison puts a voluntary government upload set beside a retailer catalog. The voluntary data.gouv.fr repairability entries average 9.22 with a 9.3 median, compared with Boulanger-listed products at 7.96 mean and 8.0 median. High scores may be legitimate, but this pattern shows why self-reported public data needs auditing, public evidence, and enough spread to help consumers compare products.

In the voluntary government dataset, 97% of products score 8/10 or higher, and only 8 of 2,173 score below 6.0.

Voluntary reporting and selection bias are likely part of the explanation, and the system still gives companies every reason to claim the highest score they can without aggressive checks on those claims. When the incentives push upward and the guardrails are weak, the result is predictable: public scores bunch at the top.

There are also direct data-quality problems: 48 repairability products and 18 durability products have conflicting scores depending on source. Smartphone manufacturers largely skipped the voluntary data.gouv.fr reporting route, except for brands such as Fairphone and Crosscall, making that category unrepresentative in the government open-data set.

Mean repairability scores from voluntary data.gouv.fr open-data uploads compared with Boulanger retailer listings. The gap suggests selection bias: companies appear more likely to publish higher scores to the voluntary government dataset.

High scores can be real. A public score dataset with little spread still needs stronger context: who reported the score, what evidence supports it, how conflicts are resolved, and what consumers can do if the promised repair access does not appear. Without that context, journalists have to chase the evidence product by product, consumers lose a comparison tool, and regulators inherit a dataset that is hard to trust at a glance.

Analysis based on score data acquired March 17, 2026 and the report's retailer comparison work.
